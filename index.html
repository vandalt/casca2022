Context

Figure: Distribution of detected exoplanets to date as a function of semi-major axis and planet mass. Jupiter-like planets have been detected at all separations, but separations beyond 1 AU remain challenging to explore compared to shorter separations.

  • Current detection methods are heavily biased towards short (transit, radial velocities) or long (direct imaging) orbits
  • Separations between 1 and 10 AU remain challenging to explore efficiently
    • RV can detect Jupiter analogs, but requires several years of coverage for each target
    • Separations below 0.1-0.2 arcsec cannot be probed with current direct imaging facilities
  • To better understand the giant planet population, we need to efficiently detect these planets
  • Combining direct detections and mass measurement would help constrain formation models for giant planets. These models are used to determine the mass of directly imaged planets.

We propose to use high dispersion spectroscopy (HDS) to directly detect thermal emission from young giant exoplanets at all separations and bridge the gap between existing detection methods.

  • The first step is to obtain a single spectrum of a star with high signal-to-noise ratio (S/N)
  • We then cross-correlate this spectrum with model spectra of potential planetary-mass companions
  • If a companion is present, the cross-correlation funciont (CCF) shows a peak, as displayed in the right panel. The peak height corresponds to detection strength.
  • Observing in the infrared maximizes the companion-to-host contrast
  • High resolution and wide spectral coverage are critical: the detection significance scales with \( \sqrt{N_\text{lines}} \) (number of spectral lines observed)

This has been shown to work for binaries with a low-mass secondary (Gullikson et al. 2016). We aim to extend it to fainter, planetary mass companions. The sensitivity of this method does not depend on orbital separation. HDS has already been used to characterize known giant planets. Once a companion is detected, it will be amenable to:

  • Radial velocity and rotational velocity (\( v \sin{i} \)) measurements
  • Mass determination and orbital characterization with follow-up observations
  • Detailed atmospheric characterization

PISCO:
Planet Inference from Spectral COrrelation

The PISCO program aims to demonstrate the proposed technique with NIRPS. It will:

  • Observe young bright stars and fast rotators
    • Bright targets ensure a high S/N with short exposures
    • Rotational broadening minimizes the spectral signature of the primary
  • Confirm performance on sky with known companions: 8 brown dwarfs and 2 giant planets with contrasts covering three orders of magnitude, from \( 2 \times 10^{-2} \) to \( 3 \times 10^{-5} \)
    • BDs will serve as clear, unambiguous demonstration of the technique
    • Opportunity to independently confirm 4 of these companions for the first time
    • Giant planets a few times more massive than Jupiter will demonstrate the feasibility of more challenging targets
  • Once the technique has been demonstrated: observe three systems suspected to host undetected giant planets at short separations
    • Synergy with JWST/NIRISS Aperture Masking Interferometry GTO program

First results

We simulated all targets from PISCO and found that all known companions can be recovered at \( 4 \sigma \) with exposures shorter than 2 hours

  • Systems with a BD secondary (contrasts \( \gt 10^{-3} \)) can be recovered at \( \gt 5 \sigma \)
  • The most challenging case, β Pictoris c (contrast \( 3 \times 10^{-5} \)), yields a \( 4 \sigma \) detection as shown below
  • For the three systems targeted by blind search, contrasts down to \( 2 \times 10^{-4} \) are attainable

Abstract

Existing exoplanet detection methods have uncovered nearly 5000 planets outside the solar system. However these methods are heavily biased towards shorter (e.g. radial velocity (RV) and transit) or longer (e.g. direct imaging) orbital separations. This leaves a range of short separations below 0.1-0.2 arcseconds that remains challenging to explore efficiently, especially with direct detection methods, and where our understanding of the planet population depends on detection limits from RV, transit and imaging. We propose a novel technique to directly detect young, giant exoplanets at all orbital separations and bridge the gap between existing detection methods. Using high dispersion spectroscopy, one can detect the spectral signature of a companion by cross-correlating observations of a star with a planetary-mass object template. Through this technique, our simulations show that companions can be detected for contrast ratios down to 3x10⁻⁵ with a single observation of less than 2 hours on a 4-meter-class telescope. Once a companion has been detected it will also be amenable to further orbital and atmospheric characterization via cross-correlation spectroscopy. We show how this technique can be used to efficiently detect hot Jupiters, young brown dwarfs and exoplanets using the SpectroPolarimètre InfraRouge (SPIRou) and the Near Infrared Planet Searcher (NIRPS). This will open the way to the efficient detection and characterization of giant exoplanet that were unattainable with current detection techniques.
