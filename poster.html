Detecting Hot Close-in Gas Giants Through Infrared High-Dispersion Spectroscopy

Thomas Vandal1 R. Doyon1 R. Allart1 É. Artigau1 J. Gagné1,2 D. Lafrenière1

1 Institut de Recherche sur les Exoplanètes, Département de Physique, Université de Montréal     2 Planétarium Rio Tinto Alcan

Abstract

Existing exoplanet detection methods have uncovered nearly 5000 planets outside the solar system. However these methods are heavily biased towards shorter (e.g. radial velocity (RV) and transit) or longer (e.g. direct imaging) orbital separations. This leaves a range of short separations below 0.1-0.2 arcseconds that remains challenging to explore efficiently, especially with direct detection methods, and where our understanding of the planet population depends on detection limits from RV, transit and imaging. We propose a novel technique to directly detect young, giant exoplanets at all orbital separations and bridge the gap between existing detection methods. Using high dispersion spectroscopy, one can detect the spectral signature of a companion by cross-correlating observations of a star with a planetary-mass object template. Through this technique, our simulations show that companions can be detected for contrast ratios down to 3x10⁻⁵ with a single observation of less than 2 hours on a 4-meter-class telescope. Once a companion has been detected it will also be amenable to further orbital and atmospheric characterization via cross-correlation spectroscopy. We show how this technique can be used to efficiently detect hot Jupiters, young brown dwarfs and exoplanets using the SpectroPolarimètre InfraRouge (SPIRou) and the Near Infrared Planet Searcher (NIRPS). This will open the way to the efficient detection and characterization of giant exoplanet that were unattainable with current detection techniques.

Orbit fitting with RV data only

We tested three sets of priors on the orbital parameters of β Pic b from direct imaging:

  • Gaussian based on values from Nielsen et al. (2020).
  • Gaussian based on values from Lagrange et al. (2019a).
  • Gaussian only in ωb and Tc,b  (in agreement for both references above).

We used the Python module RadVel (Fulton et al. 2017) to calculate RV Keplerian orbits.

Joint Fit of all RV and Relative Astrometry

  • To jointly fit RV and relative astrometry, we use the same RadVel framework as for RV only, but include an additional likelihood for relative astrometry.
  • We applie a correction to the GPI measurements (Nielsen et al. 2020). We include these corrections (in Sep. and PA) as free parameters in our model. So far, their value does not seem to affect the em to affect the final result.
  • To fit the VLTI/GRAVITY measurement from Nowak et al. (2020), we include a second custom likelihood to account for correlations between the two coordinates (ΔRA, ΔDEC).
  • Performing a joint fit of relative astrometry and RV data yields  Mb = 12.7 ± 1.3 MJup  and Mcsin(ic) = 8.5 ± 0.5 MJup.
  • Low eccentricity and period are favoured with Pb = 20.6 +1.5-0.4 yr and eb < 0.13 (3σ).
  • Generally, parameters are constrained with better precision in the joint fit than in the RV fit.
  • We use all available relative astrometry, including the VLTI/GRAVITY measurement from Nowak et al. (2020).
